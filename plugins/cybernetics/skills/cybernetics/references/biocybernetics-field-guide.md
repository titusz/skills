# Bio-Cybernetics Field Guide

Reference for scoring a subject when a rule's call is non-obvious, or when you want
Frederic Vester's original illustrations. The eight rules come from Vester's
*biokybernetische Grundregeln* — design principles distilled from how living
systems stay viable over time. Read the SKILL.md dispatch table first; come here
for depth and examples.

The eight cluster into four concerns:

- **Staying viable** — Rules 1-2 (govern growth, don't depend on it).
- **Staying flexible** — Rule 3 (serve the function, not the product).
- **Efficiency through connection** — Rules 4-7 (ride forces, multi-use, recycle, symbiose).
- **Designing with fit** — Rule 8 (feedback planning, human/nature compatibility).

## Rule 1 — Negative feedback must dominate positive feedback

- **Principle:** Self-regulation is the core organizing principle of a viable system, and the regulator is *part of* the system (the autonomic nervous system regulating hormones, a carburettor metering fuel). Anything that just keeps growing eventually swallows its surroundings and kills itself.
- **Vester's example:** the prehistoric sabre-tooth tiger. Its giant teeth never released prey, so its population grew unchecked until there was no prey left. The immediate regulating circuit burst; a *higher* regulating circuit intervened and wiped out the species.
- **Check:** Do balancing loops cap the reinforcing ones? Is there a governor inside the system, or does it rely on an external crash to stop?
- **Violation signals:** unbounded growth; "more is always better"; no circuit breaker; KPIs that only point up; success that accelerates with nothing to brake it.
- **Leverage:** install self-limiting loops *before* a higher-order loop does it for you — quotas, price signals, saturation, diminishing returns made visible, rest/recovery cycles. The regulator must live inside the system.

## Rule 2 — Vitality must be independent of quantitative growth

- **Principle:** To survive, a system must be able to *metamorphose*, not just enlarge. Past a certain size the old form is no longer viable. Connectivity matters for stability — but past an optimum, more connections reduce stability and can tip into chaos.
- **Vester's examples:** a caterpillar at a certain size is no longer viable, so it goes to zero growth at the right moment, pupates, and becomes a butterfly — sheer growth can't replace restructuring. The human brain reaches full size months after birth; continued growth there would *disturb* function.
- **Check:** Does survival depend on perpetual growth? Has added size or connectivity started to reduce stability rather than increase it?
- **Violation signals:** growth treated as the goal itself; metamorphosis avoided or feared; ever-more connections breeding fragility, latency, or chaos; "we only work if we keep expanding."
- **Leverage:** switch from *growing* to *developing* — restructure, metamorphose, change form. Seek the **optimal** (not maximal) size and degree of connection.

## Rule 3 — Function-oriented, not product-oriented

- **Principle:** Orienting to the function rather than the product gives the flexibility to survive change. The product can become obsolete; the function endures.
- **Vester's example:** the VW Beetle's breakthrough came not from the product itself but from a unique, global *service* network. Rationalization and location advantages don't help much if you won't question the product itself.
- **Check:** Is the subject's identity bound to a specific product/form, or to the function/need it serves?
- **Violation signals:** "we *are* our product"; inability to imagine surviving the product's obsolescence; optimizing the product while the need shifts underneath it.
- **Leverage:** reframe around the job-to-be-done. Ask what need persists if the current product disappears, and design for that. Dare to question the product itself.

## Rule 4 — Use existing forces (jiu-jitsu, not boxing)

- **Principle:** Work *with* available forces via leverage instead of expending energy to fight them. Asian martial arts (judo, aikido) need very little steering energy because they use the opponent's force; natural systems do the same.
- **Vester's example:** we already harness sun, water, and wind; likewise we could use any temperature gradient or waste heat — instead of fighting it with air-conditioning that demands still more energy.
- **Check:** Is the system spending energy fighting forces it could ride?
- **Violation signals:** brute-force control against a gradient (cooling the heat you just generated); fighting market momentum, incentives, or human motivation head-on; high "steering energy" for small effect.
- **Leverage:** redirect existing flows — natural gradients, waste streams, market momentum, people's existing motivations. Find the lever where a little input steers a lot.

## Rule 5 — Multiple use of products, functions, and structures

- **Principle:** Survivable systems prefer products and processes that accomplish several things at once.
- **Vester's example:** compound technology — combined heat-and-power (force-heat coupling), energy boxes, biogas utilization, insulating material and fuel made from natural fibres.
- **Check:** Do elements each serve a single purpose where they could serve several?
- **Violation signals:** single-purpose components everywhere; rigid silos; structures that do one job and sit idle otherwise.
- **Leverage:** combine functions — dual-use components, infrastructure that serves multiple roles, one team or asset covering several needs. Kill two birds with one stone.

## Rule 6 — Recycling: cyclic use of waste and byproducts

- **Principle:** Nature knows no "waste" — every product has its enzyme; every output is some other process's input. We err by valuing production over the recycling of its byproducts.
- **Vester's note:** recycling is not old news; for mid-size companies and trades it carries very attractive possibilities, precisely because most actors still treat waste as loss.
- **Check:** Are there waste or byproduct streams discarded instead of fed back in?
- **Violation signals:** linear take-make-dispose; outputs leaving the system as pure loss; heat, data, attention, or material treated as unavoidable waste.
- **Leverage:** close the loops — route every output to a process that consumes it. Look first at the byproducts you currently pay to get rid of.

## Rule 7 — Symbiosis: mutual use of difference via coupling and exchange

- **Principle:** Coupling complementary *differences* lets all participants save energy, transport, and raw material. Difference is the resource.
- **Vester's example:** the mitochondrion — a relic of a prehistoric bacterium. The cell supplies it nutrients; it manages the cell's energy supply. Both win; both save enormously.
- **Check:** Could complementary partners couple and exchange to mutual benefit? Is the subject needlessly autarkic or over-centralized?
- **Violation signals:** doing everything in-house/alone; ignoring partners whose differences are complementary; centralizing where many small local exchanges would save more.
- **Leverage:** form symbioses — pair with the *different*, not the same; favor decentralized exchange and coupling that lets each side do what it does best.

## Rule 8 — Biological design via feedback planning

- **Principle:** Everything that helps a species survive must be compatible with humans and nature — and must be designed *with feedback*, iteratively, rather than connected blindly.
- **Vester's warning:** the Internet connects anything to anybody *without structure*; unrestricted, structureless transfer lets interference and faults propagate freely — that is where the danger lurks.
- **Check:** Is the design compatible with people and the environment? Does it connect things without structure, so faults and interference spread unchecked?
- **Violation signals:** connect-everything-to-everything with no insulation or boundaries; designs hostile to the people or environment they touch; plan-then-ship with no feedback loop to correct course.
- **Leverage:** design iteratively against real feedback; add structure and insulation so a fault stays local; keep the result human- and nature-compatible by construction, not as an afterthought.

## Worked example

**Subject:** "Fix our city's congestion by adding more highway lanes."

- **Subject / function / enclosing system:** the road network; function = *mobility and access* (people reaching destinations), not lane-miles; embedded in the city's land-use and economy.
- **Feedback map:**
    - *Reinforcing loop:* more lanes → easier driving → more trips and more sprawl → more cars → congestion returns (**induced demand**).
    - *Balancing loop:* congestion itself deters marginal trips — the one governor the system has. The plan removes it.
    - *Dominant loop:* the reinforcing one, now unbraked → congestion regrows within a few years, with sprawl locked in.
- **Scorecard:**
    - **1 — Negative feedback dominates · ✗** — the plan deletes the only balancing loop (congestion-as-deterrent) and strengthens the reinforcing one. *Leverage:* restore a governor with congestion pricing.
    - **2 — Vitality independent of growth · ✗** — the "solution" is simply *more*, with no stable end state; sprawl forces ever-more building. *Leverage:* optimize access per unit of land, not lane-miles.
    - **3 — Function not product · ✗** — fixated on the product (lanes) over the function (access). *Leverage:* reframe as access — remote work, mixed-use zoning, and transit can substitute whole trips.
    - **4 — Use existing forces · ✗** — fights demand with concrete (boxing). *Leverage:* ride existing forces — pricing signals and people's own preference to avoid cost/time.
    - **5 — Multiple use · ⚠** — lanes are single-purpose. *Leverage:* dynamic lanes (bus/HOV/freight by time of day).
    - **6 — Recycling · ⚠** — consumes new land with no reuse. *Leverage:* repurpose existing right-of-way and parking for transit.
    - **7 — Symbiosis · ⚠** — the road system is treated in isolation. *Leverage:* couple with transit agencies, employers (staggered hours), and housing-near-jobs policy.
    - **8 — Feedback design · ✗** — build-then-hope, no feedback; a large interconnected build propagates the induced-demand fault city-wide. *Leverage:* pilot pricing on one corridor, measure, iterate before scaling.
- **Highest-leverage fixes:** (1) Rule 1 — restore a balancing loop via congestion pricing; (2) Rule 3 — reframe lanes → access so non-road substitutes count as wins.
- **Viability verdict:** **runaway risk / brittle growth** — without a governor, congestion returns within a few years and locks in sprawl; the dominant failure path is induced demand.
- **Watch signals:** total **vehicle-miles-travelled** after the lanes open (not peak-hour speed, which looks good briefly); land-use change along the corridor.
